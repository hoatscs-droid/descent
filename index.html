The science behind Descent

Descent is a tool for sitting quietly with focused attention. That practice, on its own, has moderate and repeatable evidence for easing anxiety and low mood. The sound is here to help you settle and stay. It is not the active ingredient. You are.

About the tones

Each stage plays a binaural beat: two slightly different tones, one per ear, create a third soft pulse you perceive inside your head. Some people find lower-frequency beats calming, and there is modest evidence for anxiety reduction, especially in the lower ranges.

The popular claim that these tones "retune your brainwaves" is not well supported. A 2023 systematic review found the evidence for brainwave entrainment inconsistent. So the honest framing is this: a stage targets a brainwave band that is associated with a state. It does not deliver the state, and it is not a shortcut around the practice itself.

The descent

The five stages move from Alpha down through Theta to Delta, the slower bands linked with relaxed and drowsy states. The names refer to those bands and nothing more.

No medical or health claims. No healing frequencies. No equivalence to any external system. Descent draws inspiration from descent-style contemplative traditions, including the Monroe Institute's Gateway framework, as inspiration only. The stage names refer to brainwave bands, not to any external system's states.

Sources to verify independently: Goyal et al., JAMA Internal Medicine, 2014 (meditation meta-analysis); Garcia-Argibay et al., Psychological Research, 2019 (binaural beats meta-analysis); Ingendoh et al., PLOS One, 2023 (brainwave entrainment review).

How to get the best from it

  • Use headphones. The binaural effect only works with a separate tone reaching each ear.
  • Set the volume low and comfortable. Quieter is better than louder here.
  • Find a still position you can hold for the whole session, seated or lying down.
  • Let the sound be background. Rest your attention on your breath or your body, and let the tone hold the edges.
  • Don't chase a state. When your mind wanders, notice, allow, and return. That returning is the practice.
  • Consistency beats intensity. A short daily sit does more than an occasional long one.
  • If anything feels destabilizing or unpleasant, stop. This is a tool, not a treatment, and it is fine to set it down.
